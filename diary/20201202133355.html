Reflect on the relationship between Abrahamic Religions

Having read and listened to this short introduction to the relationship and conflicts among the 3 Abrahamic religions, respond to the following questions.

  • Identify at least 2 things that you have learned that these 3 religions share.
  • Identify at least 2 things that you have learned that seem to condemn the 3 religions to conflict.
  • The epilogue identifies several ways in which these 3 religions might find hospitality like 3 strangers before Abraham’s tent. At the same time it recognizes that there are significant barriers. Explain why you do or don’t see hope for these 3 “children of Abraham.”
  • Respond to at least to of your colleagues in a compassionate, thoughtful and constructive way.

Essay questions

From this list of essay questions 5 will be chosen for the exam. Each question will be worth 20% of your overall grade for the exam. Your response should be two to three paragraphs in length.

  1. Choose one of 2 moral / ethical issues in our contemporary society: 1) the availability of legal and safe abortion or 2) capital punishment. Explain the difference between deontological justification of moral issues and teleological justification. Then give a justification for and against the ethical issue of your choice, using both deontological and teleological arguments.
  2. Describe the 5 pillars of Islam. Briefly explain how each pillar exemplifies one of the aspects of religion that you have learned this semester.
  3. Outline the life of Abraham as told in the Hebrew Scriptures. Describe at least 2 ways in which the Islamic stories and legends of Abraham differ from that in the Hebrew Scriptures.
  4. Using examples from the drama G-d on Trial, discuss how bringing the beit din against G-d made a kind of sense to the prisoners in the light of G-d’s covenant with Israel, his Torah which for 3,500 years breathed life into Judaism, and the threat to the survival of the people. Offer an explanation for why the prisoners prayed to G-d after finding him guilty.
  5. “I asked myself if it was only the Nazis who had persecuted us. Was it not just as wicked for people to look on quietly and without protest at human beings enduring such shocking humiliation?” (p. 57). Some of the commentators believe that those who were following orders were just as guilty as those who gave them; others, like Dith Pran, draw a moral line between followers and leaders. Would you hold them equally responsible? Why?
  6. “Without forgetting there can be no forgiving,” says retired Israeli Supreme Court Justice Moshe Bejski (p. 116); the Dalai Lama, on the other hand, believes that one must forgive but not necessarily forget. Do you think it is possible to forgive and not forget? How would you differentiate forgiveness and reconciliation?
  7. We have identified 4 basic responses that religions have made to our experience of suffering and evil. Describe and briefly explain, with illustrations, the 4 basic responses.
  8. How can ritual go wrong? What happens when it goes right?
  9. What would human life be like without the idea of sacred reality?
  10. How does mythology aid humanity in understanding their own lives?
