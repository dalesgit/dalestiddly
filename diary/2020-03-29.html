Yesterday's insight about cracks to let the light in {#Yesterday’s insight about cracks to let the light in}

Tim Fitzgerald interview. I learned that Locke was the first or in that period "politics" became a thing. Hadn't been a "thing" before. Same period saw the reification of "political economy", "economics". He claimed that the rise in 17th-18th c. of "liberalism" was in fact a move to defend the "white men" who had begun buying land. Our whole enterprise in the US is about defending the right of private ownership.

The insight I take from it is that those things that we take for granted leaves us blind. Cf. the lesson about celibacy and the evidence of laws against married clergy. We don't see it until it is pointed out to us.

Empty Signs in an Automatic Signalling System. Using words like "religion" and we think we know what they mean. The politics insight caused me to think that the mixture of religion and politics (and all the rest in culture) is in fact the norm. It was in the rise of Locke's "natural rights" of land-owners that it appeared in our tradition. But we have taken it for granted.

Locke, he said, had a stake in the "Africa Company" which was, like the "East India Company", set up for colonial exploitation. In Africa Company one found especially exploitation of the slave trade.

The Woman that she interviews on writing on the hope observes that we couldn’t see the real effects of the Soviet union breaking apart because the impact of the revolution was felt in Europe and other places where governments took it upon themselves to care more about workers and the poor immigrants and so on.￼

I’m thinking of my experience of not being able to see things that we look at directly as on the horizon.

She quotes her partner who is quoting for Karl something to the effect that we know what we do we know why we do what we do but we don’t know what we do does.

She has done significant research -- written -- about the response of people in times of disaster. Cf. Dorothy Day's formative experience of 1906 earthquake. Her own experience of 1989 earthquake. Katrina.

She finds hope coming from these events. Profound hope that often is surprising and doesn't appear for years or centuries. MLK Jr. influenced Arab Spring. Dubçek in the Prague spring of 1968 then became a leader in the 1989 fall of Soviet Union.

She notes that hope and stories of hope often feature neighbors. When the disaster collapses your house it will be your neighbors who respond.
