Dream of walking, trying to get home

I was walking and walking. At first trying to catch a bus, then I realized that I might as well just walk.

Trying to get away from the terminal of some kind – often in my dreams – I ended up in a building. Something like the Notre Dame building I couldn’t escape, but this was different. More like an apartment building in Honolulu. I was trying to get to a landing in the building that would let me out onto land. I ended up going all the way to the top. There was a pre-school that felt a whole lot like Angels at Play.

I couldn’t get out. I raced downstairs. There was someone behind me. I couldn’t believe they were keeping up. I thought it was one of the little kids. But towards the end I glanced and it was a man, obviously chasing me. But he didn’t really seem dangerous – or perhaps I just wasn’t afraid.

There had been a point at which I got to a place that I thought was a bus stop. But that’s when I thought, “I should just walk.” Yesterday I read about someone who, since the pandemic, has been walking 20,000 steps. It seemed like so much. But as I sit more and more with it I imagine that maybe 3 hours of walking a day would be comforting for me.

There are only two places where I rode the bus very often: Colorado Springs and Honolulu.

Walking out of watching TV

I have debated for days what to do when Sean came into my midst without a shirt on. Walking out. I have wondered whether that was lacking courage, passive aggressive, not being authentic. And yet I thought making a big deal about something so seemingly minimal seemed to convey something I didn’t want. Does it convey that I am insignificant? Is my desire to not rock the boat matter over everything? Or is it that what I object to his his thumbing his nose? And enforcing some rule doesn’t change that.

Same is true of the dish-washing issue. I don’t want to be a part of Mary Pat’s dishwashing crew that she orders around. I don’t want to have to bear with Sean’s standards of doing half-assed jobs. I don’t want to have to try to enforce military style rules that don’t achieve the goal of respect and conscientiousness.
